\title{Photometry and images from smart telescopes}

\section{Introduction}

Smart telescopes, such as the SeeStar S50 (etc...) are very popular and eliminate
most of the barriers to entry into astrophotography. For a relatively modest price
a smart "telescope" is the optical components of a telescope, a one-shot-color
CMOS sensor, a housing, and a basic tripod. The device takes regular, short
exposures, e.g. 10 seconds for the SeeStar S50, plate solves each exposure and
builds a stacked color image on the fly. Image taking continues until the user
stops it. The user has the option of saving only the stacked color image or the
stacked image and also of the subframes that went into the stack.

While manufacturers of these devices have focused on their use for
astrophotography, the CMOS sensors in them are adequate for performing aperture
photometry on the stars in the image. The devices typically have a wide field
of view so as to maximize the visual impact of the images they take. That implies
a fairly large pixel scale of 1-2 arcsec/pixel with a stellar full-width at half maximum (FWHM) of 3-5 pixels.

The combination of under-resolved stars and a Bayer array on the image sensor is a challenge. The traditional
approach of separating the one-shot color image into three separate lower-resolutions images, one for each
of the three color channels, will lead to severely under-resolved stars. In this paper we present an
alternative method based on applying masks to the image to select one color at a time.

In Section \ref{sec:method} we describe our methods for performing photometry on
both individual subframes and stacked images. Section XXX presents the application of these methods to a few variable stars and smart telescopes. Finally, Section YYY does SOMETHING OR ANOTHER.

\section{Methods} \label{sec:method}

There are two distinct tasks that need to be handled for each of the images: source detection/centroiding and
aperture photometry. The latter is fairly straightforward once source locations have been identified, but
the former requires some care because of the presence of the Bayer array.

\subsection{Source detection and centroid location}
Source detection and location of the centroid is performed on the full resolution image rather
than on the separate color channels. The advantages of this approach is that the stars are
better resolved than if the image were separated into three lower-resolution images.
In the end we chose to generate a source list from the Gaia catalog rather than from
the image itself, but the method described here is still relevant for centroiding
the sources in the image, which is necessary for performing aperture photometry.

\subsubsection{Handling the Bayer array for centroiding and source detection}
The presence of the Bayer array means that the background level in each color may not be uniform
across colors. An example is shown in the left column of Figure \ref{fig:color-cutout}. The image is
of the variable star T CrB taken with a SeeStar S50, relatively early in the evening when the sky
still had a significant blue cast. This image will be used extensively in the paper to illustrate our
methods because it is an extreme example of the effect of the Bayer array on the background level.

Attempting to perform source detection on the raw image will lead to large number of spurious
detections because the blue pixels are so much brighter than the red and green pixels. To
mitigate this problem, we apply what we call Bayer balancing to the image. To Bayer balance
the image, we scale each color channel's data so that the standard deviation of each color
channel matches the standard deviation of the entire image, ignoring color, and then
adjust the mean of each color channel to match the mean of the entire image. The result is
shown in the right column of Figure \ref{fig:color-cutout}. The background level is now
more uniform across colors, and source detection can be performed on the Bayer balanced
image without many spurious detections.

\subsection{Photometry on the Bayer array}

We take two new approaches to performing photometry on a Bayer array image. The first is to perform photometry
on the individual color channels by applying masks to the image to select one color at a time. The second is to
calculate a single count/magnitude for the three colors combined, which we call the `L4` channel. There are some subtleties to
correctly calculating the background and noise for the `L4` channel, which we discuss in detail below. Simply ignoring
the color information altogether and treating the image as though it were a single-channel image will lead to incorrect
results for the net counts and magnitudes.

\subsubsection{Background estimation for color channels}

The background estimation that is done for the individual filters (`TR`,
`TG`, `TB`) is based on the following formula:
\begin{equation}
C_{pp, X} = \frac{C_{tot, X}}{N_{pix, X}}
\end{equation}
Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $C_{pp, X}$ is the per-pixel background count for filter $X$.
    \item $C_{tot, X}$ is the total background count for filter $X$.
    \item $N_{pix, X}$ is the number of pixels used in the background estimation for filter $X$.
\end{itemize}

To be a little more explicit, these are, for the red, blue and green
 channels, respectively:
\begin{align}
C_{pp, R} &= \frac{C_{tot, R}}{N_{pix, R}}\\
C_{pp, G} &= \frac{C_{tot, G}}{N_{pix, G}}\\
C_{pp, B} &= \frac{C_{tot, B}}{N_{pix, B}}
\end{align}

\subsubsection{Background estimation for the `L4` channel}

For the `L4` count/magnitude the pixels are treated as though they are all
the same color, i.e. filter information is completely ignored. The
background estimation for the `L4` channel is based on the following formula:
\begin{equation}
C_{pp, L4} = \frac{C_{tot, R} + C_{tot, G} + C_{tot, B}}{N_{pix, R} + N_{pix, G} + N_{pix, B}} = \frac{C_{tot}}{N_{pix}}
\end{equation}
Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $C_{pp, L4}$ is the per-pixel background count for the `L4` channel.
    \item $C_{tot, R}$, $C_{tot, G}$, and $C_{tot, B}$ are the total background
      counts for the red, green, and blue filters, respectively.
    \item $N_{pix, R}$, $N_{pix, G}$, and $N_{pix, B}$ are the number of pixels used in the background estimation for the red, green, and blue filters, respectively.
\end{itemize}

The number of blue, green and red pixels in the background have a straightforward relationship to the total number of pixels, $N_{pix}$, that are in the background. As long as the annulus is reasonably large, $N_{pix,R} \approx N_{pix, B} \approx \frac{N_{pix}}{4}$ and $N_{pix, G} \approx \frac{N_{pix}}{2}$. This means that the `L4` background estimation can be rewritten as:
\begin{equation}
C_{pp, L4} = \frac{C_{tot, R} + C_{tot, G} + C_{tot, B}}{\frac{N_{pix}}{4} + \frac{N_{pix}}{2} + \frac{N_{pix}}{4}} = \frac{C_{tot, R} + C_{tot, G} + C_{tot, B}}{N_{pix}}.
\end{equation}

Meanwhile, the individual color channel background estimates can be rewritten in terms of the total number of pixels as:
\begin{align}
C_{pp, R} &= \frac{C_{tot, R}}{\frac{N_{pix}}{4}} = \frac{4C_{tot, R}}{N_{pix}}\\
C_{pp, G} &= \frac{C_{tot, G}}{\frac{N_{pix}}{2}} = \frac{2C_{tot, G}}{N_{pix}}\\
C_{pp, B} &= \frac{C_{tot, B}}{\frac{N_{pix}}{4}} = \frac{4C_{tot, B}}{N_{pix}}
\end{align}

Note well that $C_{pp, L4}$ will not be the same numerical value as the individual color channel background estimates. It is instead

\begin{align}
C_{pp, L4} &=    \frac{C_{tot, R} + C_{tot, G} + C_{tot, B}}{N_{pix}} \\
            &= \frac{C_{tot,R}}{N_{pix}} + \frac{C_{tot, G}}{N_{pix}} + \frac{C_{tot, B}}{N_{pix}} \\
            &= \frac{1}{4}C_{pp, R} + \frac{1}{2}C_{pp, G} + \frac{1}{4}C_{pp, B}. \label{eq:L4-background-weighted-average}
\end{align}

In other words, the `L4` background estimate is a weighted average of the individual color channel background estimates, with the weights being determined by the number of pixels used in the background estimation for each filter.

\subsubsection{Net counts in aperture}

Current calculation in `L4` channel

The net counts in the aperture for the `L4` channel are, for an aperture with $N_{ap}$ pixels, calculated as:
\begin{equation}
C_{net, L4} = C_{ap, L4} - N_{ap} \cdot C_{pp, L4}
\end{equation}
Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $C_{net, L4}$ is the net counts in the aperture for the `L4` channel.
    \item $C_{ap, L4}$ is the total counts in the aperture for the `L4` channel.
    \item $N_{ap}$ is the number of pixels in the aperture.
    \item $C_{pp, L4}$ is the per-pixel background count for the `L4` channel.
\end{itemize}

The net counts in the aperture for the individual color channels are calculated as:

\begin{align}
C_{net, R} &= C_{ap, R} - N_{ap, R} \cdot C_{pp, R}\\
C_{net, G} &= C_{ap, G} - N_{ap, G} \cdot C_{pp, G}\\
C_{net, B} &= C_{ap, B} - N_{ap, B} \cdot C_{pp, B},
\end{align}
where $N_{ap, R}$, $N_{ap, G}$, and $N_{ap, B}$ are the number of pixels in the aperture for the red, green, and blue filters, respectively, and their sum is equal to the total number of pixels in the aperture, $N_{ap}$.

Using the relationship  between `L4` background counts and single-channel background counts (Equation \ref{eq:L4-background-weighted-average}), the `L4` net counts can be rewritten as:
\begin{align}
C_{net, L4} &= C_{ap, L4} - N_{ap} \cdot C_{pp, L4} \\
            &= C_{ap, R} + C_{ap, G} + C_{ap, B} - N_{ap} \cdot \left( \frac{1}{4}C_{pp, R} + \frac{1}{2}C_{pp, G} + \frac{1}{4}C_{pp, B} \right) \\
            &= (C_{ap,R} -  \frac{N_{ap}}{4}C_{pp, R}) + (C_{ap, G} -  \frac{N_{ap}}{2}C_{pp, G}) + (C_{ap, B} -  \frac{N_{ap}}{4}C_{pp, B}) \\
            &= C_{net, R} + C_{net, G} + C_{net, B}.
\end{align}
